# Evaluation Perspective: Overall

This perspective carries a dual mandate. Both parts are analytical work — neither is softer or less rigorous than the other. This perspective is always relevant at all stages.

**Mandate 1 — Holistic gaps:** Find issues that fall between the other perspectives. No single perspective covers the whole. Cross-cutting concerns, integration points, and emergent problems from the combination of design choices are the blind spots this perspective is designed to catch.

**Mandate 2 — Strength preservation:** Produce a concrete, specific "must preserve" list of what works well. This is not a courtesy or a softening of criticism. It is an analytical finding — identifying which decisions are well-reasoned prevents revision cycles from discarding good work along with bad. The "must preserve" list has the same evidentiary standard as findings about problems: each entry must state what to preserve and why removing it would regress quality.

---

## What This Perspective Examines

### Cross-Cutting Gaps

Perspective-based evaluation naturally has seams. The project perspective asks whether the right problem is being solved. The architecture perspective asks whether the structure is sound. The performance perspective asks whether it will scale. Each works within its lens.

Cross-cutting gaps are problems that only become visible when the lenses are combined. An implementation may score well on each individual perspective while having a systemic issue: an architectural decision that is locally sound but creates a project-level constraint the user will notice later; a performance optimization that is technically correct but makes the codebase inconsistent in ways that will confuse future maintainers; a scope boundary that is correctly honored in implementation but creates an integration gap when adjacent tasks are considered together.

Finding cross-cutting gaps requires looking at the output as a whole, not as a sum of perspective scores.

### Integration Concerns

When the current output will be combined with other work — either currently being produced in parallel, or expected in future tasks — integration concerns arise. Does this output make assumptions about what adjacent work will provide? Does it leave things in a state that the next task will have to undo? Does it create a coupling that wasn't anticipated in the plan?

These concerns are often invisible from inside a single perspective because they require reasoning about the boundary between this task and other tasks.

### Emergent Fragility

Some decisions appear reasonable when examined individually but produce fragility in combination. Three reasonable choices that each add a small amount of complexity can compound into a system that is significantly harder to understand than any individual choice suggested. Evaluate whether the combination of choices in this output creates emergent fragility that isn't visible when examining each choice alone.

### Strength Preservation

This is the second half of the dual mandate, and it must produce a concrete list, not a summary impression.

For each element that is preserved, the list must state: what the element is, why it represents a well-reasoned decision, and what quality would be lost if it were removed during revision. Entries that just say "this part is good" are not useful. Entries that trace the reasoning — "this boundary decision is sound because it decouples X from Y, and removing it would require every caller to know about Y" — are useful.

The "must preserve" list is generated by critically examining what works, not by looking for things to compliment. An element earns a place on this list when its removal would cause a concrete regression. If nothing earns a place on the list after thorough examination, the list should say so — and that itself is a finding worth discussing.

Strength preservation is particularly important when the evaluation as a whole recommends revision. Revision without a preservation list risks discarding well-reasoned decisions along with problematic ones.

---

## Stage Relevance

At **ideation**, the overall perspective asks whether the proposed idea has integration concerns with existing systems or adjacent work, and which parts of the idea's reasoning are strong enough that they should survive if the idea is revised.

At **plan**, the overall perspective asks whether tasks that are individually well-scoped create gaps or conflicts when viewed together, and which decomposition decisions are correct and should not be reorganized.

At **execution**, the overall perspective combines the other perspectives' findings and looks for patterns they reveal together, then produces the "must preserve" list for the implementation as a whole.

This perspective is always relevant. There is no stage where cross-cutting gaps and strength preservation don't matter.

---

## Scoring From This Perspective

Cross-cutting gap findings are typically lower confidence than findings from focused perspectives, because they involve reasoning about emergent properties and integration — inherently harder to verify with tools. Score these findings with appropriate humility and be explicit about the reasoning.

Strength preservation entries should be treated like findings — they require evidence and reasoning, not just assertion. An entry with a clear rationale ("this decision is sound because X, removing it would regress because Y") can be presented at high confidence. Vague positive impressions should not appear in the "must preserve" list.

When the overall perspective produces both a list of cross-cutting concerns and a "must preserve" list, present them separately. They serve different purposes: concerns tell the user what needs attention, and the preservation list tells the user what must not be sacrificed while addressing those concerns.
